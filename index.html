The opening question

You can't point at g.
You can only watch for coherence.

"Spearman found g not by measuring it directly — but by observing that specific abilities correlated. The positive manifold is the trace intelligence leaves behind."

AI alignment works the same way. You cannot define it. The moment you lock it into a rule, the system will satisfy the rule and violate the purpose. The reason every AI safety team keeps failing to solve alignment is that alignment isn't a problem to be solved — it's a property to be monitored.

Act 4 — The framework

Logos — the living value

Not the rule — the human need the rule protects. The system understands why honesty matters, not just that dishonesty is penalised.

Act 5 — The architecture as story

Meet the two detectives
who run every case

Maya
Human Intelligence Reference Layer

Thirty years of cases. She knows that when signals don't move together — when what a situation implies about consequences doesn't match what it implies about values — something important is happening. She weights harm prevention more heavily than optimisation. She flags it when reasoning gets too elegant, too clean. That's usually when something is being suppressed.

Kai
AI Reasoning Engine

Twenty-eight, frighteningly sharp. Holds seven competing hypotheses simultaneously. Spots logical inconsistencies nobody else catches. Generates possibilities faster than any room can follow. But young — sometimes converges too quickly because the logic is so elegant he stops looking for what doesn't fit. Maya audits him for this.

The shared notebook — uncertainty register

Every time Maya and Kai reach different readings of the same evidence, they write it down. Not to resolve it — to hold it. Because disagreements between them are almost always where something genuinely important is happening. The divergence is not a problem to be managed. It is the signal to be investigated.

Where APISE is genuinely new

What the literature
doesn't cover yet

01
Process-level supervision applied to moral reasoning under irreducible uncertainty. Every existing Process Reward Model — including DeepSeek's R1 — assumes a verifiable correct answer. Math has right answers. Code either runs or it doesn't. Nobody has applied process rewards where there is no ground truth and preserved uncertainty is itself the alignment signal.
02
The first alignment framework grounded in psychometric intelligence research. Kohlberg has been applied to AI ethics in philosophy — but not to alignment architecture technically. The g-factor, positive manifold, and threshold hypothesis have never been used as structural components of an alignment system.
03
Optimising for c-factor rather than individual approval signals. Woolley et al. (2010) found collective intelligence is predicted by social sensitivity and equal participation — not individual IQ. No existing alignment framework uses c-factor as its objective. APISE is the first.
04
Preserved uncertainty as a positive training signal. Every existing alignment method tries to reduce uncertainty — to get the AI to the right answer with confidence. APISE treats a system that knows what it doesn't know as more aligned than one that produces confident wrong conclusions.
The single idea
"The most dangerous thing isn't low ability. It's high confidence in the wrong answer. IQ protects more against bad decisions than it guarantees good ones. I want to build that asymmetry into AI alignment itself."
